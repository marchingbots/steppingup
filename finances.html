Financial Management



Overview

Regardless of practice size, all successful firms have one thing in common. They are organised businesses with consistently implemented systems and procedures that ensure the practice attracts clients and matters, completes the work efficiently and expeditiously, prices the work above cost and collects debtors promptly.


Concepts

1. Pricing - Billing Rate

The following approaches are used in high-performing firms to ensure lawyers achieve their hourly budgets:
• Meeting daily with junior lawyers to help them plan their day – the open door policy works well in conjunction with structured daily meetings, but is a poor substitute;
• Reviewing the file load of all lawyers to ensure they have properly prioritised their time and that no critical dates slip through the net;
• A one-on-one meeting each week with each fee-earner to discuss objective and subjective performance issues;
• Six-monthly file closing days where all lawyers bring their difficult files. The group as a whole work through each file with the aim of closing or advancing as many as possible. In addition to the obvious financial benefits, there is a positive impact on lawyer stress levels as their difficult files have been turned into fees.


2. Realisation

a measure of the percentage of the work done (ie work-in-progress or WIP) which is actually billed to clients.

eg. For example: assume you spent 10 hours working on a matter at a rate of $300 per hour and you invoiced the client $2,500; your realisation would be calculated as: $2,500/(10 hours x $300) = 83.33%

3. Margin Management

A well-leveraged practice will most likely have a salaries margin (excluding the equity principals) of greater than 40%. This will usually result in a profit margin (before principals’ salaries) of less than 30%. The overall profitability of such a practice in terms of net profit per principal will be quite high as the profit is shared among fewer principals.


4. Pricing, Cost of Production, and discounting

In the business of legal practice one of the major differentiators between the most profitable and those less so has always been price.

solicitors underpricing is their desire to justify price objectively. Unfortunately, most practices do not have the data to enable them to make these decisions accurately, so they make them anecdotally and incorrectly.

In a law office, record time.
Total overheads (including break-even salary for partners) DIVIDED by total chargeable hours = COST PER CHARGEABLE HOUR.
The average profit margin (after a commercial salary for equity principals) for small law practices is around 12.25%. If you are average and you put your fees up by 6%, you could achieve a 50% increase in profit. Even if you lost some clients you will still be much better off.

This is where the psychology of pricing comes into the argument. If you are going to be expensive you must be good. All of those things that you know influence satisfaction should be excellent. You must perform. You must perform on or ahead of time. You and your staff must be pleasant to deal with. You must be totally client-focused at all times.

regs 9 and 10 of LCA trust account regs

No debiting of fees prior to work: A lawyer may not take fees for work which is yet to be completed – reg 10 of the LCA (Trust Account) Regulations 2008.
Estimates: If requested, a lawyer must provide an estimate of fees – r 9.4.
Estimate exceeded: A lawyer must inform the client promptly if it becomes apparent that the fee estimate is likely to be exceeded – r 9.4.
Legal aid: A lawyer must advise a client if legal aid might be available (although a lawyer may also indicate that they are not prepared to work on legal aid) – r 9.5.
Prompt final account: A lawyer must render a final account to the client or person charged within a reasonable time of concluding a matter or the retainer being otherwise terminated – r 9.6.
Reporting with account: The lawyer must provide sufficient information to identify the matter, the period to which it relates and the work undertaken with the final account – r 9.6.
Conditional fees: If a conditional fee arrangement is entered into, particular rules (rr 9.8 – 9.12) must be complied with (see the further discussion in this section).
Fee sharing: It is misconduct to share a fee with a non-lawyer pursuant to s 7(3) of the LCA (this is an effective prohibition on multidisciplinary practices). Fee sharing with other lawyers is permitted only where the lawyers have worked on the matter or the split is due to the sale of a legal practice (see RCCC, rr 9.13 and 9.14).
Fees of lawyers selling real estate: Particular rules relating to lawyers selling real estate are found in rr 16.3 to 16.6 of the RCCC. Most importantly, lawyers may not charge a percentage commission when selling real estate – Real Estate Agents Act 1976, s 3(7).

Respecting Confidency and Privacy

r 8 RCCC

There is a difference between confidentiality and privacy. Confidentiality is about the secrecy and security of information whereas privacy is about the way personal information is collected, stored, accessed, kept accurate, retained, used, and disclosed. They co-exist as important professional obligations.

What is duty of confidentiality?
The duty extends beyond merely not divulging but also extends to protecting the information. This means that a lawyer may have to take positive steps to protect information (for example, in the use of email or other internet security measures).

Duration: Duty extends indefinitely.
