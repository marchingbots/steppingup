LCA 2006

Disciplinary standards:
- misconduct s 7 LCA

- unsatisfactory conduct s 12 LCA

RCCC
- r. 1.5.2 : preservation of dignity and repuation of legal profession is responsibility of every lawyer.

regulatory regime focuses on complaints resolution rather than professional discipline.
- lawyers must have their own complaint resolution mechanism per s. 94(j)(iv).
- explicit recognition some complaints can be resolved by negotiation or mediation per ss 143 and 201.
- s 138: if an aggrieved person does not want to pursue the matter, this is grounds for the standards committee to decide to take no action

Competence is a professional standard per s 12 LCA.

Responsibilities to the Client

1. Client Care


At heart of client care is COMMUNICATION.
Alongside this is CLIENT CONTROL.

2. Nature of the Relationship


s 94(j) LCA: lawyers must "provide clients in advance with information on the principal aspects of client service."

This flows to r 3.4 RCCC: a lawyer must, in advance, provide a client in writing the principal aspects of client service including the following:
a. basis of fees
b. professional indemnity arrangements of lawyer's practice
c. coverage provided by Lawyer's Fidelity Fund and if the client's funds are held/utilised for purposes not covered by it
d. procedures in lawyer's practice for handling of compalints by clients, advice on existence and availability of law society's complaint process, and how law society may be contacted.

3. In Advance


In McGuire v Manawatu Standards Committee21 the Court considered the use of the word “recommendation” in footnote 3 to r 3.4, significantly softened the mandatory nature of r 3.4. Arguably, what is intended in r 3.4 is that the disclosures required by that rule must occur prior to undertaking significant work under a retainer.

Info to be provided in advance


1. Principal aspects of client service
- r 3.4 gives non-exhaustive examples: a. basis upon which fees will be charged,
b. when fees must be paid,
c. if lawyer intends to deduct fees from the trust account - client must be informed in advance (ss 9 and 10 of LCA trust account regs), ;
d. lawyer's procedure for handling complaints by clients
e. how a client complaints to law society ;

Rule 3.5 RCCC provides additional info to that required under s 94(j) LCA:
a. copy of client care and service information;
b. name and status of persons who will have general carriage of, or responsiblity for, the work;
c. any provision that limits extent of lawyer's obligation to the client, or limits or excludes liabilty

Matter Management

1. Identify client's needs.

2. Gather the facts
- open ended questions
- listening to answers: CONTENT, WILL, EMOTION

3. Consider the law

4. Identify the options
- from doing nothing to the most radical option

5. Make decisions
- assess pros/cons of each option
- decide what is best for the client outcome
- THE CLIENT DECIDES
- SUCCESS = leadership by managing and moving along the decision process
- give client pros/cons of each option

6. Carry out instructions
- create plan to achieve client's objective
- identify costs and timeframe
- record in writing
- have client sign them as your instructions to proceed

7. Report to client, terminate retainer, close file
- final report with document copies for client
- checklist of future actions they must undertake
- terminating file is good risk management practice


File Management

File management system hardwires managing client's instructions.

A standard system will:
- enable anyone to pick up the file and know where to locate the info they need.

Letter of Engagement:
It sets out:
- who the client is;
- the client instructions;
- outline of my actions;
- how client will be charged for services provided; and
- expected timeframe, any special terms such as limitation of liability agreed to.

Identify and exceed client expectation.
- set longer timeframe, deliver before that = client happy


The Cost Dimension: QUOTATIONS

- Rule 9.4 RCCC: firm must provide estimate if requested AND inform client if fee estimate will be exceeded.

- give client control over how they spend their money:
break a client's instructions into steps
- Rule 9 RCCC: requirement for a fee to be fair and reasonable (unreasonable fee factors in r9.1)

FEE ARRAGNEMENTS:
1. Blended hourly rates
- one rate no matter who works on the file

2. Fixed Fees
- risk of cost blowout passes from client to the firm
- price the job taking the risk factor into account

3. Hybrid Time and Fixed fees
- where part of the task is uncertain but once completed, leads to more certainty

4. Capped Fees
- charging on an hourly basis with a cap

5. Retainers
- specified fee for services over a specific period
- a fee to secure the exclusive and immediate services of a lawyer with actual work charged for seperately OR
- will include the work.

6. Success Fees and Reduced Fees
- fees charged if certain agreed results are achived on top of another form of charging
- reduced fee is the reverse

7. Contingency Fee
- lawyers only paid if they achieve a predetermined successful outcome or there are court awarded costs.

8. Conditional Fee Agreements
LCA, ss 333 to 336 and RCCC, rr 9.11 and 9.12 deal with conditional fee agreements

9. Value Billing
- based on what the task is worth to the client
